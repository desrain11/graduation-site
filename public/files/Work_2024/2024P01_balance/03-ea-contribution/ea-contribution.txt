During this semester, guided by my vision for everyday well-being and my personal interest in designing tools for daily use, I established a graduation project collaboration with the external partner Nexo Intelligent Technology Limited. Within this collaboration, I developed the product Hola from concept to early implementation, designing the interface from zero to one. My responsibilities included clarifying client requirements, defining the functional architecture, and designing interfaces and interactions for key intervention-related features. At the same time, I defined a UX research direction grounded in the project’s current commercial objectives, which will continue into the Final Major Project (FMP).
At the early stage of the project, I reviewed existing user research insights provided by the client and discussed current development requirements and progress. By studying the Elements of User Experience model and mapping the client’s development stage onto this framework, I gained a clearer understanding of the fundamental process of developing a commercial digital product from zero to one, which was subsequently applied in later design iterations.
Through ongoing collaboration with experts from different departments, several technical and practical issues that are often difficult to identify from a purely design perspective were revealed and discussed. For example, when designing personalized chatbot responses, we examined how abstract personalization concepts could be translated into concrete and trainable parameters based on the current capabilities of intelligent agents. In addition, practical insights emerged around intentionally limiting user control over certain personalization parameters, to prevent Hola from becoming merely a reflection of users’ immediate preferences.
After confirming personalized chatbot responses as one of the product’s key competitive advantages, I conducted further analysis through industry cases and academic literature. This helped me refine the research focus around ‘everyday use chatbots’, ‘response personalization’, and ‘sustained self-disclosure’. Based on this process, I formulated a clear research question and initial hypothesis: how different levels of user controllability over personalization for a chatbot’s communication style influence Anticipated Communication Quality (ACQ) and Communication Willingness (CW) in self-disclosure scenarios. This approach differed from my previous individual research project in M12, where research questions were primarily derived from user insights and a single design hypothesis. In contrast, a collaborative product with multiple features required identifying the most critical UX issues at the current product stage. During this process, the Business Model Growth Map was used to support analysis and discussion.
In terms of prototype develop skills, I was responsible for developing high-fidelity Figma prototypes from V2-P0 to V2-MVP, covering the complete application flow from login to all core functional interfaces and navigation structures. At the V2-P0 stage, expert review helped confirm a restructured information architecture and core interaction logic, addressing earlier issues related to unclear functional hierarchy. The V2-MVP prototype focused on supporting alignment between front-end and back-end development and enabled external participants to provide early user feedback before full product implementation.
In addition, based on the current development stage and available technical support, I designed a research-oriented prototype for chatbot response personalization. I recruited six participants aged 18 to 35, matching the target user group, to conduct UX research. Participants evaluated both the personalization prototype and the usability of the MVP interface, providing qualitative and quantitative feedback. The findings informed further product iteration for the company and also generated new insights into personalization content and controllability, contributing directly to my design research on chatbot response personalization.
Overall, this semester allowed me to engage with all five Expertise Areas through practical application. Compared to the previous semester, in which my experience in Business and Entrepreneurship was relatively limited, the M21 project provided valuable opportunities to develop new design and research perspectives through collaboration with an external company. This experience aligns closely with my intention to pursue a career in industry and with the objectives of the Research, Design and Development (RDD) track. The project also strongly reflects my vision of creating lightweight, playful, and everyday-integrated experiences that encourage users to re-examine their daily lives. Through this process, I was able to engage with AI-driven experience design, and I aim to continue exploring how AI can be integrated into everyday contexts to create meaningful changes in user experience in the future.
