The DUIET has enhanced my capabilities in three key dimensions: design thinking, implementation methods, and design research. My main takeaways can be summarized in the following five points: a new understanding of user interface concepts, pathways and methods for learning about the latest advancements in user interfaces, designing and developing with CV Marker technology, a technology-driven perspective on design, and approaches to designing with new technologies within broader societal issues.
To begin with, the course introduced me to an open-source approach to knowledge sharing and skill acquisition, which fundamentally empowered me to continuously improve my technical understanding and engage in design research more effectively. As the course progressed, I gained a deeper understanding of how technological developments are driving changes in user interface design. My previous focus on GUI within the WIMP paradigm, influenced by market demand, was broadened after exploring more research and case studies. User interfaces are not merely about aesthetically pleasing digital screens; they can integrate multi-sensory dimensions to enhance the interaction between users and computers or other intelligent devices.
Additionally, the individual assignment pushed me beyond my comfort zone of using visual effects to simulate system functionality. Using CV Markers for my design project marked my first attempt at incorporating programming tools into design development. Building on the sample programs provided by Professor Rong-Hao and leveraging ChatGPT for assistance, I successfully completed the programming aspects of LumiTile. This experience gave me greater confidence to independently develop interactive prototypes in the future. However, reflecting on the Midterm Demo Day, I realized that my focus on achieving the desired interactive effects overshadowed broader considerations of how the technology could be applied to real-life scenarios. This realization led to a shift in approach during the group challenge. After discussing with my teammates, we revisited our foundation paper and decided to explore a more suitable application scenario for HERMIT technology—cooking.
In our group challenge, I took the main responsibilities to planning and organizing user testing, acting as the system for interactions that were difficult to implement at the current stage, such as automatically notifying users of task completion and providing voice guidance. These responsibilities provided me with invaluable opportunities to practice organizing user tests, experimenting with the Wizard of Oz technique, and learning observation and analysis methods. These were areas I had not fully explored in previous projects. However, this experience also highlighted areas for improvement, particularly in optimizing my simulation of system interactions during Wizard of Oz testing. A more seamless execution would help minimize the impact of human presence on participants’ perceptions of the product being tested.
Throughout the course, the challenges shifted my perspective from focusing on new technologies to considering their integration into users’ daily lives. While technological innovation can undoubtedly transform product design methods and concepts, meaningful design is rooted in societal observation and reflection. Technology validates ideas, but design is what enables technology to find its rightful place in society.
