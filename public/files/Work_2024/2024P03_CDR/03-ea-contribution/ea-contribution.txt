This project mainly contributed to my development in Design Research Process. Through a studio-based Constructive Design Research approach, I learned how design knowledge can be generated through making, testing, and reflecting, rather than only through predefined research methods. I treated the language toolkit and interface prototypes as research materials, using iterative design journeys to explore how LLM-based support could affect multilingual collaboration in offline meetings. This helped me understand the role of prototypes not only as design outcomes, but also as tools for inquiry, discussion, and knowledge generation. The project also strengthened my ability to translate messy design explorations into research insights, such as the distinction between public screens for shared attention and private screens for personalized language support. This direction is consistent with the project’s use of Studio methodology, where knowledge was developed through rapid prototyping, testing, and multiple design journeys.

EA: Technology & Realization

The project also contributed to Technology & Realization through the exploration of LLM and NLP-based meeting support. I worked with interface concepts around translation, prompts, summaries, meeting language settings, and different screen configurations. Although the project did not focus on building a fully deployable product, it helped me understand how technical possibilities need to be translated into interaction structures. For example, we explored how LLM functions should be distributed between private and public screens, and how screen type influences the feasibility and appropriateness of different functions.

EA: User & Society

The project contributed to User & Society by focusing on international students’ meeting experiences and the social effects of language-support technologies. The research addressed how cultural background, language confidence, privacy, and meeting etiquette influence users’ expectations of collaborative tools. In particular, the findings showed that private screens are better suited for personalized language support, while public screens can support collective awareness and reduce distraction when used carefully.
